Maitrī: Practice & Resources

Loving-kindness, the Metta Sutta, and the boundless heart

Etymology and the Four Divine Abodes

Maitrī comes from the Sanskrit root mitra, meaning friend or companion — the quality of genuine, reliable goodwill that characterizes a true friendship. The Pali equivalent is mettā, from mitta (friend). Both forms point to the same essential quality: not the intense, possessive warmth of romantic love or even familial attachment, but the steady, non-possessive goodwill of a true friend — one who wishes you well without needing anything from you in return.

Maitrī is the first of the four Brahma-vihāras (divine abodes, or immeasurable qualities): maitrī/mettā (loving-kindness), karuṇā (compassion), muditā (appreciative joy — joy in others' happiness), and upekkhā/upekṣā (equanimity — the quality that makes the other three sustainable). These four together represent the fullest expression of the awakened heart — not abstract ideals but concrete qualities that can be cultivated through practice.

The Metta Sutta

The Karaṇīyamettā Sutta (Sn 1.8), commonly called the Metta Sutta, is one of the most beloved texts in all of Buddhism. Attributed to the Buddha, it is recited daily in Theravāda Buddhist communities around the world and is among the first texts learned by many practitioners. It is a short text — fewer than thirty verses — that describes first the qualities of the person who practices maitrī, and then offers the practice itself: the boundless radiation of loving-kindness toward all beings in all directions.

The central image of the Metta Sutta is among the most powerful in Buddhist literature: "As a mother protects with her life her own child, her only child — so with a boundless heart should one cherish all living beings." This is the scope of maitrī: not limited to those we know, not conditional on their behavior, not diminished by distance or difficulty. It begins with the specific and expands until it holds everything.

Practice: Beginning Metta Meditation

The formal practice of metta meditation (loving-kindness meditation) is one of the most widely taught Buddhist practices in the world today, and for good reason: it is accessible, deeply nourishing, and produces measurable changes in mood, outlook, and even the capacity for empathy over time. Here is a simple, traditional approach.

Step 1: Arrive and Settle

Find a comfortable seat. Take a few easy breaths. Let the body settle and the mind begin to quiet. There is no need to be perfectly calm — simply arrive as you are.

Step 2: Begin With Yourself

Place your hand on your heart, if that feels natural. Silently offer yourself the traditional phrases of metta — adapting the words so they feel genuine, not forced:

May I be well. May I be happy. May I be at peace. May I be free from unnecessary suffering.

If you find it difficult to direct goodwill toward yourself, you are not doing the practice wrong — this difficulty is why the practice begins here. Simply stay with the intention, even when the feeling is elusive. The direction matters more than the emotional temperature.

Step 3: Extend to a Beloved Being

Call to mind someone you love easily and naturally — a child, a close friend, a pet, a teacher, anyone in whose presence you feel genuine warmth. Offer them the same phrases: May you be well. May you be happy. May you be at peace. Let the feeling of goodwill toward them be genuine and simple.

Step 4: Extend Further

Gradually extend the practice to: a neutral person (someone you see regularly but don't know well), a difficult person (someone with whom you have friction or conflict — start with mild difficulty), and then all beings without exception. The traditional final direction is: "all beings everywhere, without limit — may they be well, may they be happy, may they be free."

A Note on the Practice

Metta meditation is not about performing warmth you don't feel, or forcing positive emotion. It is about orienting the intention toward genuine well-wishing — and trusting that the feeling follows the direction, gradually and in its own time. Five minutes a day, practiced consistently, is more valuable than an hour practiced occasionally with great effort.

Texts & Teachings

  • Karaṇīyamettā Sutta (Sn 1.8) — The Metta Sutta Pali Canon · Sutta Nipāta

    The foundational text on maitrī/mettā practice. Short enough to be memorized, deep enough to practice with for a lifetime. Available in multiple translations; those by Bhikkhu Sujato and Bhikkhu Ṭhānissaro are widely used. The full text is available freely at accesstoinsight.org and suttacentral.net.

  • Metta Sutta Discourse (AN 11.15) Pali Canon · Aṅguttara Nikāya

    A companion discourse to the Metta Sutta, describing the eleven benefits of practicing loving-kindness. One of the most frequently cited texts in metta meditation instruction.

  • Visuddhimagga (Path of Purification), Chapter 9 Theravāda · Classical commentary · Buddhaghosa, 5th century CE

    Buddhaghosa's detailed instructions for the formal practice of metta as a concentration meditation (samatha bhāvanā), including the systematic extension of loving-kindness to all beings in all directions. The most comprehensive classical treatment of the metta practice.

  • Śāntideva's Bodhicaryāvatāra, Chapters 5–8 Mahāyāna · Classical text

    Chapters on mindfulness, patience, and the practice of exchanging self and other — the Mahāyāna elaboration of maitrī into a comprehensive practice of universal love. Chapters 8 (on meditation and equalizing self with others) is particularly relevant.

Further Learning

  • Loving-Kindness: The Revolutionary Art of Happiness Book · Sharon Salzberg

    Sharon Salzberg's foundational book on metta meditation is one of the most accessible and practically useful introductions to the practice available in English. Rooted in years of Theravāda practice and teaching in the West, it offers both the theory and the practice of maitrī in a form suitable for contemporary lay practitioners.

  • The Four Immeasurables: Practices to Open the Heart Book · B. Alan Wallace

    A thorough treatment of all four Brahma-vihāras — including maitrī — from a Tibetan Buddhist perspective, with historical context and guided practice instructions. Useful for those who want to understand the full framework of which loving-kindness is one part.

  • Access to Insight: Metta Resources Online library · Free

    The website accesstoinsight.org provides free access to the Metta Sutta, the Visuddhimagga chapter on metta, and numerous related essays and translations. One of the most reliable free resources for Theravāda Buddhist texts in English.

  • Self-Compassion Research and Practice Suggested category · Psychology & practice

    Dr. Kristin Neff's work on self-compassion (self-compassion.org) draws directly from Buddhist maitrī practice and brings it into a psychological and scientific framework. Her research has demonstrated the measurable benefits of treating oneself with the same kindness one would offer a good friend.
