Karuṇā: Meaning & History

Compassion, Guanyin, and the courage to meet suffering with care

Etymology and Sanskrit Roots

Karuṇā derives from the Sanskrit root kṛ, meaning to do, to act, to make — combined with a suffix that indicates a state of feeling or being moved. The word carries the sense of being acted upon by the suffering of another: moved, stirred, impelled toward response. In Pali the word is identical: karuṇā, and it appears throughout the Buddhist scriptures as one of the highest qualities of the awakened mind.

In classical Buddhist psychology, karuṇā is carefully distinguished from domanassa (grief or distress) — its "near enemy," the response that looks like compassion but is actually a form of suffering. True karuṇā does not collapse into the suffering of the other. It remains present, stable, and responsive. It is the compassion of the physician who is moved by the patient's pain but does not become ill themselves — and therefore can actually help.

Guanyin: The Bodhisattva of Compassion

Guanshiyin (觀世音) — most commonly known as Guanyin (觀音) in Chinese, Kannon in Japanese, Gwaneum in Korean, and Quan Âm in Vietnamese — is the East Asian manifestation of the Bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara (He Who Perceives the Sounds of the World). Avalokiteśvara is the great Bodhisattva of Compassion in Mahāyāna Buddhism, perhaps the most widely venerated figure in all of Buddhist Asia.

The name Guanyin itself is a rendering of Guanshiyin: one who perceives (guan) the sounds (yin) of the world (shi). In the Universal Gateway Chapter (Chapter 25) of the Lotus Sūtra — one of the most beloved texts in all of East Asian Buddhism — the Buddha describes Avalokiteśvara as one who perceives the cries of all suffering beings in all realms and responds immediately with compassionate aid, taking whatever form is most useful to help. This quality of unconditional, immediate, responsive presence is the heart of karuṇā as embodied by Guanyin.

Over centuries, as the Bodhisattva's cult spread from India through Central Asia into China, Korea, Japan, and Southeast Asia, Avalokiteśvara underwent a gradual gender transformation in East Asian iconography. While appearing as male in Indian and Tibetan art, Guanyin in Chinese tradition became increasingly feminine from approximately the 10th century CE onward — eventually becoming one of the most female figures in Buddhist iconography, associated with maternal compassion, birth, and the protection of the vulnerable.

Practice & Reflection

Compassion Meditation (Karuṇā Bhāvanā)

The formal practice of compassion meditation follows a similar structure to loving-kindness meditation, but focuses specifically on suffering: the wish that beings be free from suffering and the causes of suffering.

Begin by settling into stillness. Then bring to mind someone who is suffering in a way that is easy to hold — not the most overwhelming suffering you can imagine, but someone whose pain you can acknowledge without being overwhelmed by it. Silently offer: May you be free from suffering. May you find relief. May you be at ease.

Gradually extend this to: yourself (acknowledging your own suffering with the same gentleness), others you know who are suffering, and eventually all beings who suffer anywhere. Let the practice be gentle. Compassion does not require you to take on suffering; it requires you to acknowledge it and wish for its relief.

The Practice of Presence

In daily life, one of the most direct expressions of karuṇā is simply the willingness to be fully present with someone in pain — without rushing to fix, to advise, to minimize, or to move away. This is harder than it sounds. Most of us, in the face of suffering, feel an impulse to do something, to say something that makes it better. Karuṇā sometimes means remaining still, saying very little, and trusting that genuine presence — the simple fact of not turning away — is itself a profound form of care.

Sustaining Compassion: Avoiding Burnout

Compassion fatigue is a real phenomenon: the depletion of caregivers who give without also receiving care, who hold others' pain without ever putting it down. The Buddhist tradition addresses this directly through the teaching on equanimity (upekkhā) — the fourth Brahma-vihāra, which gives compassion its ground. Genuine karuṇā includes care for the caregiver. Sustainable compassion requires rest, boundaries, self-care, and the honest acknowledgment that you cannot help anyone if you have nothing left to give.

Texts & Teachings

  • Lotus Sūtra, Chapter 25: The Universal Gateway of Guanshiyin Mahāyāna · Classical sūtra

    The foundational text for Guanyin devotion in East Asia. Chapter 25 describes Avalokiteśvara's thirty-three transformations and unconditional responsiveness to all suffering. This chapter is often chanted as a standalone devotional text in Chinese Buddhist communities and is the most direct scriptural basis for the Lotus Karuṇā figurine.

  • Heart Sūtra (Prajñāpāramitā Hṛdaya) Mahāyāna · Classical sūtra

    One of the most chanted texts in Mahāyāna Buddhism, and the text in which Avalokiteśvara (Guanyin) famously teaches the wisdom of emptiness to the monk Śāriputra. The pairing of compassion (Guanyin) and wisdom (prajñā) is central to the Mahāyāna understanding of the enlightened heart.

  • Karaṇīyamettā Sutta — see Maitrī resources Pali Canon · Companion reading

    The Metta Sutta, while focused on loving-kindness, provides the immediate context for karuṇā in the Brahma-vihāra system. Reading the two texts together illuminates the relationship between love (maitrī) and compassion (karuṇā) in Buddhist practice.

Further Learning

  • Comfortable with Uncertainty Book · Pema Chödrön

    Pema Chödrön's work on tonglen — the Tibetan Buddhist practice of taking in suffering and sending out relief — is one of the most accessible introductions to karuṇā practice available in English. Her approach is rooted in the Mahāyāna tradition and is particularly helpful for those who feel overwhelmed by suffering (their own or others').

  • Guanyin: The Chinese Transformation of Avalokiteśvara Book · Chün-fang Yü

    A thorough scholarly study of how the Bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara became the feminine Guanyin of Chinese Buddhist tradition. Provides historical, iconographic, and literary context for understanding the Lotus Karuṇā figurine within its full cultural and religious heritage.

  • Tonglen: The Practice of Giving and Receiving Suggested category · Tibetan Buddhist practice

    Tonglen (Tibetan: giving and taking) is the Tibetan Buddhist compassion practice of breathing in the suffering of others and breathing out relief. While different in form from karuṇā bhāvanā in the Theravāda tradition, it points to the same quality of the compassionate heart. Teachings are widely available through Shambhala publications and Pema Chödrön's recorded talks.
